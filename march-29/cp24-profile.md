New Democrats selected Avi Lewis to lead their party, tasking him with rebuilding a caucus that has lost official status in Parliament and charting a path back to federal prominence.

Lewis took the stage to crowd chants and called on all supporters to unite. Manitoba Premier Wab Kinew stood behind him onstage, and Lewis called him a "political powerhouse."

"Our party is back and our tent is growing," Lewis said from the podium after securing 56 percent support with 39,734 members' votes.

"Canadians are living on the edge. We're under economic attack by the U.S., while Donald Trump stomps around the globe grabbing foreign leaders and oil fields" starting conflicts, Lewis stated. The audience responded with "Shame!"

Lewis criticized both major parties for bending to billionaires' interests rather than holding CEOs accountable.

## Background

The Vancouver-based documentary filmmaker and author pitched himself as a leader who could form a government "that serves the many, not the money." He was largely seen as a front-runner and pledged to disrupt Canada's wealth concentration.

Lewis ran unsuccessfully for federal office twice — in West Vancouver–Sunshine Coast–Sea-to-Sky in 2021 and Vancouver Centre in 2025, placing third both times.

His media experience includes hosting CBC's "CounterSpin" and co-creating Al Jazeera's "Fault Lines." He conducted interviews with David Bowie and Leonard Cohen for CityTV's "The New Music."

Lewis co-founded the Leap Manifesto, a 15-point plan for Canada to "transition beyond fossil fuels." The document quickly divided New Democrats, particularly in energy-rich western provinces.

He teaches climate justice and documentary film at the University of British Columbia and lives in Vancouver with his wife, author Naomi Klein, their son Toma, and dog Smoke.

## Major Policy Proposals

Lewis's campaign centred on seven pillars addressing inequality, climate change, healthcare, and fascism. Major proposals include:

- A wealth tax on Canada's top 1 percent estimated to generate $40 billion annually
- National rent caps and a "Federal Housing Secretariat" to coordinate home-building
- Ending federal approvals for new pipelines and natural gas projects
- A tax on oil and gas exports to counter U.S. tariffs
- Pausing AI data center expansion in Canada
- Free public transit, high-speed rail, and electric bus services
- Investing 2 percent of GDP in climate action, allegedly creating over one million union jobs
- A public grocery alternative using a warehouse model to reduce costs by up to 40 percent
- A public pharmaceutical manufacturer for domestic vaccine production

## Political Opposition

Not all New Democrats celebrated Lewis's victory.

Alberta NDP Leader Naheed Nenshi stated: "someone who openly cheered for the defeat of the Alberta NDP government, is not in the interests of Alberta." He emphasized Alberta's belief in energy jobs and pipelines.

Saskatchewan NDP Leader Carla Beck said she would meet with Lewis only if he changes his position on resource development, calling his stance "ideological and unrealistic." She cited 40,000 Saskatchewan jobs tied to natural resource development.

Former NDP leader Tom Mulcair highlighted Nenshi's "strong condemnation," noting Lewis must appeal to resource-producing provinces to win seats nationally.

## Challenges Ahead

Lewis faces significant obstacles. The NDP entered last year's election with 24 seats, lost ground to just seven members, and recently lost Nunavut MP Lori Idlout to the Liberals. The party needs 12 seats for official status — double its current number.

A March 25 Angus Reid poll found 44 percent of former NDP voters didn't recognize any leadership candidates. About 25 percent said the party is irrelevant, and 40 percent believe its best days have passed. More than one-fifth disagreed that the NDP represents the working class, with many now voting Conservative.
